Big, heavy, and slow?


Going fast on the cups, actually takes a lot of skill. It really doesn't matter how strong you are. In fact the bigger you are the heavier you are. The heavier you are the more drag there is on the bearings, the more weight there is to accelerate, and the more likely you are to have the weight off center, which loads the bearings more causing more drag. All these cause enough drag to overpower the extra force the puller might have. Anthony was a pretty big guy and his arms really didn't move all that fast. He had a lot of force on the bottom end, but once the cup got moving someone with longer arms had to get in and pull. Les weighs like around 100 lbs and has toothpicks for arms. I weigh 135 and don't have much more, yet he and I were in the fastest cup ever recorded. A .50 with 2 dead people inside. Now that's not to say it's not really cool riding with the equivalent of a 426 hemi. Once me and Michigan Torque went an a run together and when he started out I actually slid across the seat with each pull! That was cool! But we didn't go all that fast. There was just too much drag. It's cool riding with someone real big once in a while, but (as in my racing) I prefer the equivalent of little 2-strokes. That's the fastest way. I haven't seen what the cups have for bearings yet. I don't know if they are plain, or ball, or roller. They feel like they have plastic ball bearings when they are worn out and shake the cup, but it could be because the gel coat plain bearings have crumbled. If the bearings were ball or roller it really wouldn't matter how much you weigh except for accelerating which only accounts for about an estimated 30% of the mid speed drag.
