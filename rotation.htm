Pull the left side of the handle toward you

This is actually very important to go fast. There are two turrets that the cups are on of relevance here. First There's the major turret. On the major turret, there sit 3 minor turrets. On each minor turret sit 5 cups (so how many cups does the ride have). But as far as each individual cup is concerned, there is a major, and minor turret. I mean who cares how fast the other guys riding the cups go? The major turret spins COUNTER clockwise when your looking down at it. The minor turret spins clockwise.

The major turret doesn't rotate all that fast, rpm wise, so it really doesn't have that much effect here (it does effect Gs though). But the minor turret spins at something like approximately 8 rpm (very approximate). At a 1.00 you are spinning 60rpm at which the minor turret is spinning at 13%, or at 120rpm, the fastest that TTC has ever been clocked, .50, the minor turret is spinning at 7% of the cup speed. So considering that the handle is connected to it, it is relevant. However because we are talking about two different directions, the ratio of effectiveness over the speed you go is doubled. When you spin counter clockwise, (again when looking down) the handle seems to move much faster while you actually aren't moving very fast at all. So it takes more horsepower to turn it. The definition of horsepower is speed times force. The equation to figure out horsepower is torque in foot lbs * rpm / ~5252. Knowing that you know that the faster the handle is moving the more horsepower, or energy, it takes to spin the cup. So the slower the handle is moving in relation to you the longer you can pull. Also the faster a muscle moves, the less force it can put out. Something bicyclists know all too well. As a result, the slower the handle moves in relation to you the harder you can pull it.

Another factor is that when you spin the cup backwards the bearings are moving faster, so there is more drag. As a result it takes more force to speed the cup up. Something you don't have anyway at the speeds your arm is moving.

We typically go around .75 going clockwise and .85-.90 going counter clockwise.
